Executive Summary: Caldor Fire Land Cover Change Analysis

Prior to the 2021 Caldor Fire, the study area was characterized by a high density of Evergreen Forest, which accounted for 86.72% of the total land cover in 2020. Analysis of post-fire data from 2022 indicates a landscape-scale conversion of these forested areas. By 2022, Grassland/Herbaceous vegetation became the dominant land cover class, representing 80.04% of the study area. Spatial patterns in the 2022 map show a near-total loss of contiguous forest cover across the central and eastern portions of the perimeter, replaced by early-successional herbaceous cover.

As of 2024, the study area remains dominated by early-successional vegetation, with Grassland/Herbaceous cover persisting as the primary land cover class. While the 2024 spatial data indicates a fragmented re-emergence of forest cover—notably along the western and northern perimeters—the interior of the burn scar has not returned to pre-fire forest densities. The transition analysis from 2020 to 2024 confirms that the primary landscape-scale shift was the conversion of Evergreen Forest to Grassland/Herbaceous cover, a state that remains largely stable three years post-fire.

Pre-Fire Land Cover (2020)

The second most prevalent category was Shrub/Scrub at 10.06%, representing the only other land cover class exceeding 2% of the total area. Together, these two classes comprised nearly 97% of the landscape, indicating a high degree of fuel continuity prior to the 2021 ignition.

Spatially, the Evergreen Forest is distributed uniformly throughout the central and western portions of the study area. Shrub/Scrub communities are primarily concentrated in the eastern and southeastern sections, with smaller pockets interspersed within the forest matrix. Grassland/Herbaceous cover (1.4%) and Deciduous Forest (0.13%) are minimal and appear as fragmented patches rather than contiguous stands.

Anthropogenic footprints and hydrologic features comprise a small fraction of the landscape. Developed land covers—primarily Developed, Open Space (1.15%)—are scattered along the western perimeter and central-northern corridors. Combined, all developed categories total approximately 1.43%. Aquatic and wetland environments, including Open Water, Woody Wetlands, and Emergent Herbaceous Wetlands, collectively represent less than 0.3% of the pre-fire landscape.

Post-Fire Land Cover (2022)

The current distribution suggests a persistent shrub/grassland state typical of mid-term post-fire succession, with forest regeneration limited to specific western corridors.

Land Cover Transitions (2020-2024)

Land Cover Transitions (2020-2024)

Between 2020 and 2022, the study area underwent a primary landscape-scale transition from Evergreen Forest to Grassland/Herbaceous cover. Data indicates that 71.53% of the total area transitioned from forest to grassland, while only 14.85% of Evergreen Forest remained stable. During this same interval, 6.85% of the area transitioned from Shrub/Scrub to Grassland/Herbaceous. Developed classes, including Open Space and Low Intensity, remained minimal and stable, collectively accounting for approximately 1.3% of the landscape.

From 2022 to 2024, the rate of transition slowed, with Grassland/Herbaceous persisting as the dominant land cover at 57.69%. However, a notable successional trend emerged as 20.24% of the Grassland/Herbaceous class transitioned to Shrub/Scrub. A smaller portion (1.88%) of grassland transitioned back to Evergreen Forest. Evergreen Forest stability increased during this period, with 13.8% of the area maintaining forest cover. Developed categories showed negligible expansion, with Developed Open Space increasing slightly to 1.1%.

Spatially, the landscape is characterized by a broad distribution of Grassland/Herbaceous cover that forms the primary matrix of the study area.
